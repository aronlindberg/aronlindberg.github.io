About

Background

Aron Lindberg is an Associate Professor of Information Systems and Chair of the Information Systems & Analytics area at the School of Business, Stevens Institute of Technology. He received his Ph.D. from Case Western Reserve University's Weatherhead School of Management and holds a guest professorship at Umeå University in Sweden.

Most organizations treat AI as a productivity tool. Aron's research starts from a different premise: the most consequential question is not whether AI can do the job, but what humans and AI can create together that neither could produce alone. His work examines how organizations design the roles, workflows, and structures that make human-AI partnerships genuinely generative, with a focus on creativity, innovation, and complex problem-solving.

His work appears in MIS Quarterly, Information Systems Research, and Organization Science. He serves as Associate Editor at MIS Quarterly and Senior Editor at the European Journal of Information Systems.

Research

Three Intertwined Streams

A single question runs through all of Aron's research: how do humans and organizations think and create differently when AI is part of the process? The three streams below examine that question from different angles.

01

AI-Enabled Innovation

The productive question is not whether AI can do the job but what humans and AI can build together that neither could produce alone. This research examines human-AI collaboration as a design problem: how organizations structure roles, workflows, and decision rights to turn AI capability into creative output and genuine innovation.

02

Physical-Digital Innovation

When a product becomes digital, who can make it, how it is made, and what it can do all change at once. This research examines how the boundary between physical and digital artifacts shifts as organizations bring AI and computational tools into design and production, and what that shift means for who participates in innovation.

03

Computational Knowledge Tools & Methods

This research builds systems and artifacts that use large language models to generate knowledge, surface insights, and construct theories of how organizations work. The goal is not to automate analysis but to create tools that extend what researchers and practitioners can know, and to understand how AI-powered knowledge artifacts change what questions become answerable.
